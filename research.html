Age-Related Changes in Emotion Processing

Understanding how emotion recognition and representation develop across the lifespan

← Back to Profile

Overview

Emotions seem to function as a signal to the mind that behaviour needs to be adjusted or maintained. Sometimes this concerns the environment directly: to flee a predator, or to approach a source of food. Other times it concerns whether to trust or hold back, with cooperation reinforced by positive feeling and mistrust signalled by its absence. And sometimes it shapes social conduct, reinforced through pride or corrected through shame and guilt. In this way, emotions seem central to both social life between individuals and to a person's own inner regulation.

This is the idea that first drew me into studying emotions, during my undergraduate and Master's years in psychology and philosophy.

Research Questions

Alongside this broader interest, I became drawn to questions of when particular emotions emerge across human development, and to questions of universality. At what age do we begin to experience and recognise emotions in ourselves and in others? Are my emotions the same as those of a stranger I encounter?

These questions have guided my academic path ever since. My undergraduate thesis examined the concept of shame. My Master's theses investigated guilt and its relationship to personal identity. I then worked in the Department of Psychiatry in Zurich, helping to develop a digital intervention aimed at preventing depression through reappraisal strategies. Finally, in my doctoral research at the University of Oxford, I became increasingly drawn to developmental changes in emotion processing — spanning understanding, recognition, and the visual concepts people hold of emotions.

Key Influences

A handful of papers in particular have shaped my thinking and guided my own work.

Widen & Russell: Developmental Acquisition

Probably the most influential is the work of Sherri Widen and James Russell, who demonstrated that children's recognition of facial expressions of emotion develops gradually, following predictable patterns of acquisition. This foundational work showed me that emotion recognition is not innate but rather emerges through developmental stages.

Jack & Colleagues: Mental Representations

I was also intrigued by the approach taken by Rachael Jack and colleagues, who had participants view computer-generated random facial movements and judge which emotion, if any, each pattern conveyed. Across many trials, they used reverse correlation to identify the key movement patterns associated with each emotion. Critically, this approach offers a window into individual and cultural differences in the mental representation of emotion.

Long & Fan: Drawing as a Method

This led me to want a method for measuring children's changing emotion representations in a way that goes beyond simple correct/incorrect outcomes. I came across the work of Bría Long and Judith Fan, who used drawing as a method for tracking the development of visual concepts for object categories, and decided to adapt this approach to emotional expression.

Methodology

I wanted to see, first, whether the relationship between facial expression and contextual information (body posture, situational context) changes across development, and second, whether more fine-grained, small-scale changes in visual features could be tracked over time.

Over the following two years, I built a custom-coded drawing interface and collected drawings of ten emotions — happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, pride, jealousy, shame, guilt, and embarrassment — from both adults and children.
